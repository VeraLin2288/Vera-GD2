Haiku has its origins in Japan, evolving from earlier poetic forms such as renga and haikai no renga. The haiku, as we recognize it today, began to take shape in the 17th century. One of the most famous haiku poets is Matsuo Bashō (1644–1694), who elevated the form to its pinnacle and greatly influenced its development.



Initially, haiku were part of collaborative linked-verse poetry (renga), with the opening stanza called hokku. Over time, hokku began to be composed as standalone poems, and it eventually became known as haiku. Bashō and other poets of his era refined the haiku into a concise and evocative form, often drawing inspiration from nature and the seasons.



During the Edo period (1603–1868), haiku became increasingly popular among the Japanese populace. They were appreciated for their simplicity, immediacy, and ability to capture fleeting moments in nature. Haiku also gained recognition as a form of contemplative poetry, reflecting Zen Buddhist principles of mindfulness and enlightenment.



Haiku underwent further evolution in the modern era, both in Japan and internationally. Poets outside of Japan began to adopt the form, sometimes adapting it to suit their own cultural contexts. In the West, haiku has become a popular form of poetry, often written in English and following the 5-7-5 syllable structure, though this strict adherence to syllable count is not always observed in contemporary haiku.



Overall, haiku continues to be celebrated as a poetic form that captures the essence of nature, human experience, and the passage of time in just a few succinct lines. Its rich history and adaptability have contributed to its enduring popularity both in Japan and around the world.
